CO₂ Transport Resilience

ETH Zürich - Zurich, Switzerland

Fall 2025

Problem: CO₂ transport networks are vulnerable to infrastructure failures, which can significantly increase both cost and emissions depending on the available mitigation strategy.

Approach: Developed a techno-economic optimization framework modeling multiple transport options (pipeline, truck, rail, ship), temporary storage, and system constraints. The model evaluates system performance under different failure durations, transport distances, and carbon pricing scenarios.

Result: The analysis shows that resilience strategies are not universally optimal. Temporary CO₂ storage emerges as the most robust solution across a wide range of scenarios, while alternative transport modes become competitive under specific distance and disruption conditions. The Pareto front highlights the trade-off between cost and emissions across different configurations.

  • Modeled multi-modal transport systems (pipeline, truck, trains, ship, barges)
  • Integrated carbon pricing and failure scenarios
  • Analyzed cost–emission trade-offs

Tools: Python, Network Modeling

Mega Heat Pump Economic Evaluation

ETH Zürich × Everllence (ex MAN Energy Solutions)

Fall 2024 - Spring 2025

Problem: Large-scale heat pumps can provide flexibility services to the grid, but their economic viability depends on how effectively they can participate in electricity balancing markets (FCR).

Approach: Developed a techno-economic model integrating thermodynamic system behavior with electricity market data. The model evaluates different operational strategies, including participation in FCR markets and system oversizing, to determine optimal dispatch and profitability.

Result: FCR revenues exhibit strong temporal variability, creating opportunities for flexible operation strategies. Oversizing the heat pump enables participation in these markets, increasing system utilization and improving overall economic performance compared to standard operation.

  • Integrated thermodynamic modeling with electricity market participation
  • Evaluated system performance across different sizing strategies
  • Quantified the value of flexibility and grid services

Tools: Python, Thermodynamic Modeling, Energy Markets
